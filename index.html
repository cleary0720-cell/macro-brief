Economic Output

+1.6%
EXPANDING ▲
The BEA's second estimate for Q1 2026 GDP, released May 28, revised growth down to +1.6% annualized from the advance estimate of +2.0% — a 0.4 percentage point reduction driven primarily by weaker investment and consumer spending than initially measured. Exports and government spending provided partial offsets. The economy is still expanding and avoided contraction, but the revision signals that first-quarter momentum was softer than first reported.
In plain termsGDP measures the total output of the US economy. At +1.6% annualized growth in Q1 2026 — revised down from +2.0% — the economy is still expanding, but more modestly than first estimated. Businesses are still growing and hiring; there is no recession signal here, but the pace is decelerating.
Fixed Income
10-Year Treasury Yield
4.45%
EASING ↓
The 10-year Treasury yield settled at 4.45% on May 29 — the last trading session before the Memorial Day holiday — easing from its cycle high of 4.59% on May 15 and falling from 4.56% the prior week. The decline reflects modest relief following the GDP second estimate revision to +1.6% and some softening in near-term rate hike expectations. Still elevated relative to the post-pandemic norm, but the directional move provides marginal improvement for borrowers ahead of the June 17 FOMC decision.
In plain termsThe 10-year Treasury drives mortgage rates. At 4.45%, a 30-year fixed mortgage likely costs around 6.9% — meaning a $400,000 home loan runs approximately $2,625 per month in principal and interest. That is down slightly from recent peak levels, but still far above the sub-4% rates that prevailed before the Fed's tightening cycle.

With CPI and PCE both running at 3.8% year-over-year in April, money supply growing comfortably above the 2–3% range associated with price stability remains a central obstacle on the Fed's path back to 2%.
In plain termsM2 measures how much money is flowing through the economy — cash, checking accounts, savings, and money market funds. At $22.8 trillion and growing 4.7% year-over-year, there is a lot of money chasing goods, which keeps prices elevated even as the Fed holds borrowing costs high.

Editor's Analysis

GDP Revised to +1.6% as PCE Confirms 3.8% Inflation — the June 17 Decision Crystallizes

With jobs on June 5, CPI on June 10, and the Fed's rate decision on June 17 — the defining fortnight of 2026 macro policy is now underway — By Connor Leary, May 31, 2026

The Bureau of Economic Analysis delivered a double-header on May 28: the second estimate for Q1 2026 GDP and the April PCE price index, released simultaneously at 8:30 a.m. The GDP revision to +1.6% annualized — down 0.4 percentage points from the advance estimate of +2.0%, driven by weaker investment and consumer spending than initially measured — arrived alongside PCE inflation of 3.8% year-over-year and core PCE of 3.3%. The combined picture is the defining macro tension of mid-2026: an economy softening at precisely the moment when inflation remains stubbornly resistant. For the Federal Reserve, which has held its target range at 3.50–3.75% through three consecutive meetings in 2026, neither data point offers a clear path forward.

The 10-year Treasury yield processed the GDP revision with measured relief, easing to 4.45% on May 29 — the last trading session before the Memorial Day holiday — from 4.56% the prior week and below the May 15 cycle high of 4.59%. The market's interpretation is consistent with the Fed's calculus: slower growth reduces the urgency of a rate hike, but does not eliminate it. What has changed is the slope of the probability distribution around the June 17 outcome — a hike is somewhat less likely than it was two weeks ago, but far from removed. With CPI and PCE both printing at 3.8% for April, the Fed has now received two independent confirmations that inflation is not transitory, not narrowing, and not cooperating with its 2% mandate.

"With PCE and CPI both printing at 3.8% for April, the Fed has received two independent confirmations that inflation is not transitory, not narrowing, and not cooperating with its 2% mandate — the June 17 meeting is a live decision."

The June 17 FOMC meeting will be framed by an unusually dense data calendar. The May employment situation arrives June 5, May CPI on June 10, and advance May retail sales on the morning of June 17 itself — the same day the committee announces its decision. This sequencing is consequential: the Fed will be absorbing labor market, inflation, and consumer spending data in real time as it deliberates. The committee has not signaled a strong lean in either direction, leaving the outcome genuinely dependent on the incoming numbers. Chair Powell's post-meeting press conference will be scrutinized for any recalibration of the easing bias that cost three FOMC members their assent at the April meeting.

The labor market data arriving June 5 will be particularly important context for interpreting June 10 CPI. April's 115,000 payroll print was accompanied by average hourly earnings growth of 3.6% year-over-year — above the Fed's comfort zone but not accelerating. If May jobs continue to decelerate modestly while wage growth stays sticky, the labor market will send the same mixed signal it has dispatched since the spring: softening volume, persistent price. That combination keeps both the hold and hike options simultaneously viable. The Fed cannot look past 3.8% CPI simply because payroll growth is decelerating; price stability currently commands the higher weight in the dual mandate.

The M2 picture adds a structural dimension to the near-term data sprint. The Fed's May 26 H.6 release showed M2 expanding 4.7% year-over-year through April, with total money supply reaching $22.8 trillion. That growth rate — comfortably above the 2–3% range historically associated with price stability — provides the monetary fuel for inflation's persistence. Aggregate liquidity is growing faster than the economy's productive capacity, and the Fed's current rate stance has not yet fully absorbed it. This is the underlying mechanism that makes the last mile to 2% so difficult to cover: the structural problem has not changed while the cyclical debate about hikes versus holds dominates the conversation.

The retail sector remains the clearest counterargument to pessimism. April sales of $757.1 billion — up 4.9% year-over-year — confirm that the US consumer has not retreated despite high borrowing costs, a softening job market, and persistent inflation. What distinguishes this moment from the 2022–2023 tightening cycle is that the economy is not clearly overheating — growth at 1.6%, unemployment at 4.3%, and consumer spending robust but not surging. But neither is inflation at 3.8% a condition the Fed can accept. Whether May retail data on June 17 signals continued momentum or a first crack in the consumer foundation is the last data point before the committee decides.
