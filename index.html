Key Economic Indicators
JUNE 2026
Monetary Policy
Federal Funds Rate
3.63%
ON HOLD →
The FOMC voted unanimously 12-0 on June 17 to hold the target range at 3.50–3.75% — the fourth consecutive hold and the first decision under new Chair Kevin Warsh. The real shock was the dot plot: the year-end median jumped to 3.8% from 3.4% in March, implying at least one 25 basis-point rate hike before year-end. Nine of 18 participating members now project at least one increase in 2026, with six seeing two or more. Warsh declined to submit a dot himself — consistent with his stated aversion to forward guidance — but the committee's hawkish pivot is unambiguous. Markets now price October as the earliest plausible timing for the first hike.
In plain termsThe Fed held rates unchanged on June 17, but its updated projections now show most officials believe rates may need to go HIGHER — not just stay flat. That means mortgages, car loans, and credit card rates could get more expensive before they get cheaper. The next scheduled meeting is July 29, 2026, where markets now price a 19% chance of a rate hike.
Inflation ⚠ WATCH
Consumer Price Index (YoY)
4.2%
SURGING ↑
May 2026 CPI rose 4.2% year-over-year — the highest reading since April 2023 — driven overwhelmingly by energy prices, which jumped 23.5% on an annual basis amid Iran-related geopolitical supply disruptions. Core CPI (excluding food and energy) came in at 2.9%, only modestly above April's 2.8%, confirming that demand-driven price pressure remains relatively contained. Shelter costs rose 3.4% YoY. The FOMC held rates on June 17 as expected — treating the energy spike as a supply shock — but the dot plot's hawkish tilt indicates 9 of 18 members believe the cumulative inflation picture still justifies tightening. The June 25 BEA release will provide the first look at May Core PCE, the Fed's preferred gauge.
In plain termsPrices are rising at 4.2% — more than twice the Fed's 2% goal — but most of that surge comes from gas and energy costs driven by the Iran-Israel conflict. Strip out energy and the picture is less alarming. Still, groceries, rent, and everyday expenses are all meaningfully more expensive than a year ago. The Fed held rates on June 17 but its own officials now project rates may need to rise further to tame persistent underlying inflation.
Labor Market
Unemployment Rate
4.3%
STABLE →
May payrolls added 172,000 jobs — beating every economist estimate and the strongest monthly print since spring 2024 — with gains concentrated in leisure and hospitality, local government, and health care. The unemployment rate held at 4.3% for the third consecutive month, with 7.3 million unemployed and labor force participation steady at 61.8%. Upward revisions added 93,000 jobs to March and April combined. Average hourly earnings moderated to 3.4% year-over-year, a slight improvement that eases near-term wage-price spiral concerns — but the acceleration in hiring complicates the Fed's messaging ahead of the June 17 decision.
In plain termsThe job market is holding up better than most economists expected. With 172,000 jobs added in May and the unemployment rate steady at 4.3%, layoffs are not accelerating and most employed workers are keeping their jobs. The strong hiring pace actually makes it harder for the Fed to cut rates — a hot labor market means less economic slack and more inflationary pressure.
Economic Output
GDP Growth Rate (Q1 2026, 2nd Est.)
+1.6%
EXPANDING ▲
The BEA's second estimate for Q1 2026 GDP, released May 28, revised growth down to +1.6% annualized from the advance estimate of +2.0% — a 0.4 percentage point reduction driven by weaker investment and consumer spending than initially measured. The economy is still expanding and avoided contraction, but the revision signals that first-quarter momentum was softer than first reported. The third and final Q1 2026 estimate is scheduled for June 25, alongside May Core PCE — both releases will influence the market's view of whether a July 29 hike is justified.
In plain termsGDP measures the total output of the US economy. At +1.6% annualized growth in Q1 2026 — revised down from +2.0% — the economy is still expanding but more modestly than first estimated. No recession signal here, though the pace is decelerating. The final Q1 GDP estimate arrives June 25 and is unlikely to change the picture significantly.
Fixed Income
10-Year Treasury Yield
4.45%
ELEVATED →
The 10-year Treasury yield fell to 4.45% on June 18 — a counterintuitive 4-basis-point decline on the day after the hawkish June 17 FOMC meeting. The front end told a starkly different story: the 2-year yield surged 17 basis points on June 17 alone, its biggest single-day move on a Fed day since March 2008, as markets priced near-term hike risk from the hawkish dot plot. The resulting 2s10s spread narrowed to +27 basis points from +40, and the 3m10y spread eased to +70 basis points — a flattening pattern consistent with markets pricing short-term hikes but skeptical that long-run growth can sustain them.
In plain termsThe 10-year Treasury yield sets the floor for mortgage rates. At 4.45%, a 30-year fixed mortgage likely costs around 6.9–7.0% — meaning a $400,000 home loan runs approximately $2,641–$2,661 per month in principal and interest. While the 10-year itself edged lower this week, the 2-year yield spiked sharply after the hawkish FOMC dot plot, signaling that near-term borrowing costs could rise further if the Fed hikes in October.
Consumer Activity
Retail Sales Growth (YoY)
6.9%
SURGING ↑
May 2026 advance retail and food services sales totaled $763.7 billion — up 6.9% year-over-year and 0.9% from April, the fourth consecutive monthly gain and the strongest annual growth rate in over a year. Nonstore retailers led with a 12.2% year-over-year jump as e-commerce continued to capture spending share. Total sales for March through May are up 5.3% from the same period a year ago. Released on the same morning as the FOMC decision, the May retail data confirmed the American consumer has so far refused to be deterred by 4.2% inflation, elevated borrowing costs, or geopolitical uncertainty.
In plain termsAmericans stepped up their spending sharply in May — retail sales grew 6.9% year-over-year, the strongest pace in over a year. Despite 4.2% inflation and expensive credit, consumers are not pulling back. Online shopping alone surged 12.2%. The strength is encouraging (no consumer recession) but also gives the Fed reason to keep rates elevated: a spending economy doesn't need monetary support, and it reduces the urgency for rate cuts.
Money Supply
M2 Money Supply Growth (YoY)
4.7%
EDGING HIGHER ↑
M2 expanded 4.7% year-over-year through April 2026 — revised upward from the initial H.6 estimate of 4.5% — per the Federal Reserve's latest data. Total money supply stands at approximately $22.8 trillion. The upward revision marks a modest re-acceleration from March's 4.6% growth rate, and the trend suggests monetary conditions remain loosely accommodative despite the Fed's extended pause at 3.50–3.75%. Above-trend M2 growth provides structural support for consumer spending and elevated price levels, helping explain why inflation has proved so persistent.
In plain termsM2 measures all the money circulating through the economy — cash, checking accounts, savings, and money market funds. At 4.7% annual growth, there is still more money chasing goods than the economy can absorb — a key reason inflation hasn't returned to the Fed's 2% target. The upward revision from 4.5% is a small warning sign: monetary conditions are still accommodative enough to sustain elevated prices.
Inflation
Core PCE Price Index (YoY)
3.3%
RE-ACCELERATING ↑
April 2026 Core PCE — the Fed's preferred inflation gauge — rose 3.3% year-over-year, up from 3.2% in March and well above the 2% target. After bottoming near 2.6% in early 2025, core prices have climbed steadily for more than a year as service-sector and shelter costs prove persistent.
In plain termsThe Fed's favorite inflation yardstick — it strips out food and energy to measure the "core" of price pressure. At 3.3%, it's well above their 2% goal and has been creeping higher for over a year. This is the number that most directly influences whether the Fed raises or cuts interest rates.

ISM Manufacturing PMI
54.0
EXPANDING ▲
May 2026 ISM Manufacturing PMI surged to 54.0 — the strongest reading since May 2022 — capping a dramatic reversal from 10 consecutive months of contraction through 2025. After bottoming at 47.9 in December 2025, manufacturing has expanded for five straight months as new orders and production accelerate.
In plain termsA monthly survey of factory purchasing managers — any reading above 50 means manufacturing is growing, below 50 means it's shrinking. At 54.0, U.S. factories are expanding at their fastest pace in four years. Strong manufacturing usually signals more jobs and higher wages ahead.
Labor Market
Initial Jobless Claims (4-wk avg)
223k
EDGING HIGHER ↑
The 4-week moving average of initial jobless claims rose to 223,250 for the week ending June 13, 2026 — reported June 19 — up from 219,000 the prior week and continuing a gradual drift higher from the cycle low near 200,000 reached in early May. The ongoing uptick extends a now six-week trend of gently rising claims, though at 223,250 the level remains well below the 250,000–275,000 threshold historically associated with recessionary conditions. A continued climb into July could begin to soften the Fed's hawkish posture, but the current level is consistent with a labor market still adding jobs at a solid pace.
In plain termsEvery week, the government counts how many people filed for unemployment benefits for the first time. At 223,000, layoffs are slowly ticking up from recent lows but remain historically low. This six-week upward drift is worth watching: if claims keep rising into July, it could signal the labor market is softening — which would reduce the pressure for the Fed to hike rates and possibly revive the case for eventual cuts.

Editor's Analysis

The Warsh Fed's Hawkish Turn: Dot Plot Signals 2026 Hike as Retail Sales Surge to +6.9%

Nine FOMC members now project at least one 2026 rate increase — the rate-cut window has definitively closed — By Connor Leary, June 21, 2026

The June 17 FOMC meeting delivered what markets expected on the surface — a unanimous hold at 3.50–3.75% — but the dot plot underneath told a starkly different story. The year-end median rate projection jumped to 3.8% from 3.4% in March, a full quarter-point higher and the largest single-meeting dot plot revision in years. Nine of 18 participating members now project at least one 25 basis-point rate hike before year-end, with six seeing two or more. This is not the committee maintaining patience; it is a committee signaling that the next move is up. The rate-cut window — which markets were still debating as recently as April — has definitively closed.

Chair Kevin Warsh, presiding over his first FOMC meeting since replacing Jerome Powell in March, declined to submit a dot himself. Warsh has long been a critic of forward guidance, arguing that numerical projections create more market distortions than they resolve. But his silence on the dot does not soften the message from his colleagues. In the post-decision press conference, Warsh characterized the committee as "data-dependent and attentive" — careful language that gives the Fed maximum flexibility to hike in July, October, or not at all, depending on incoming data. Markets, reading the dot plot and Warsh's tone together, have price October as the most likely timing for the first increase.

"Nine policymakers who participated in the exercise projected at least one hike, with six even suggesting multiple hikes could be in the offing."

The bond market's reaction was immediate and revealing. The 2-year Treasury yield surged 17 basis points on June 17 — its biggest single-day move on a Fed day since March 2008 — as traders repriced the near-term rate path. Yet the 10-year yield fell 4 basis points on the same day, finishing at 4.45%. The divergence tells a precise story: the front end believes the Fed will hike; the long end believes that tightening will ultimately slow the economy and bring rates back down. The 2s10s spread narrowed from +40 to +27 basis points in a single session — a curve flattening that, in prior cycles, has often served as an early-warning yellow flag for growth prospects 12–18 months ahead.

Against this backdrop, the May retail sales data released the same morning as the FOMC decision provided the most striking counterpoint. Advance retail and food services sales totaled $763.7 billion in May — up 6.9% year-over-year and 0.9% from April — the strongest annual growth rate in over a year. Nonstore retailers surged 12.2%. The message from American households is unmistakable: despite 4.2% inflation, 7% mortgage rates, and a labor market showing the first gentle signs of cooling in jobless claims, consumers are spending at an accelerating pace. This spending strength is precisely what makes the Fed's hawkish pivot analytically coherent — you only hike rates into a consumer that can absorb it.

The three critical data points before the July 29 FOMC meeting will substantially determine whether the first hike arrives in October or gets pulled forward. June 25 brings both the final Q1 GDP estimate and May Core PCE — the Fed's preferred inflation gauge, last reading 3.3%. If May Core PCE accelerates above 3.5%, the arguments for an early hike strengthen considerably. The June employment situation on July 2 then provides the labor market read, and June CPI on July 14 gives the final inflation picture before the committee reconvenes. A trifecta of hot data — PCE acceleration, strong jobs, sticky CPI — could force the first hike as early as July 29, where markets currently price only 19% probability.

The longer structural picture remains the most important frame. The Warsh Fed has inherited an economy that is, by most conventional measures, performing well: +1.6% GDP growth, 4.3% unemployment, 54.0 ISM Manufacturing, and now +6.9% retail sales. Inflation at 4.2% is the problem — and notably, the dot plot's PCE inflation projection for year-end was revised up to 3.6% from 2.7%. That revision implies the committee no longer believes inflation will self-correct to target without intervention. For ordinary borrowers, the message is sobering: the most plausible path for mortgage rates, car loans, and credit card rates is not down but sideways or higher, as the Fed moves from patience to active management. The experiment of "higher-for-longer" may be giving way to "higher-still."
