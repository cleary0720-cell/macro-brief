Fixed income markets continue to price a meaningful probability of a Fed rate hike at the June 17 meeting.
In plain termsThe 10-year Treasury drives mortgage rates. At 4.56%, a 30-year fixed mortgage likely costs just above 7% — meaning a $400,000 home loan runs roughly $2,650 per month in principal and interest, and refinancing into a lower rate remains out of reach for most homeowners.

In plain termsAmericans stepped up their spending in April, growing faster than inflation. This is the economy's strongest positive signal: consumers are not pulling back despite high prices, rising yields, and global uncertainty.
Money Supply
M2 Money Supply Growth (YoY)
4.6%
ACCELERATING ↑
M2 expanded 4.6% year-over-year through March 2026, with total money supply reaching $22.7 trillion per the Fed's April 28 H.6 release. The sustained acceleration in money growth provides a structural underpinning for both the GDP rebound and the persistence of inflation — it represents excess liquidity that the Fed's current rate stance has not yet fully absorbed, and it helps explain why the "last mile" to 2% is proving so difficult to cover.
In plain termsM2 measures how much money is flowing through the economy — cash, checking accounts, savings, and money market funds. At $22.7 trillion and growing 4.6%, there is a lot of money chasing goods, which keeps prices elevated even as the Fed holds borrowing costs high.

Editor's Analysis

FOMC Minutes Confirm Vigilance as the June 17 Decision Comes Into Focus

With PCE on May 28, jobs on June 5, and CPI on June 10 — the data sprint to the most consequential FOMC meeting in over a year is now underway — By Connor Leary, May 24, 2026

The 10-year Treasury yield stood at 4.56% on May 22 — a fractional pullback from the 4.59% peak registered on May 15, but no lasting relief for a bond market still absorbing the cumulative weight of persistent inflation, an Iran conflict driving energy prices roughly 60% above pre-war levels, and a Federal Reserve that has clearly stopped talking about rate cuts. The FOMC minutes from the April 28–29 meeting, released Tuesday, May 20, confirmed what markets had largely already priced in: the committee is watching the inflation outlook with heightened vigilance, the threshold for a rate hike has moved meaningfully closer, and the June 17 meeting is now a live decision.

The minutes landed at a delicate moment for the Fed's communications strategy. GDP is expanding at 2.0% annualized in Q1 2026. The labor market added 115,000 jobs in April, with unemployment holding at 4.3% — not recessionary conditions. But CPI at 3.8% year-over-year, core CPI at 2.8%, and an energy shock with no clear resolution horizon have compressed the room for patience. The committee that spent much of 2025 preparing markets for rate cuts is now contemplating the opposite. The speed of that reversal — from three cuts executed in late 2025 to hike risk by mid-2026 — reflects the Iran conflict's disruptive power over an already fragile inflation trajectory.

"The June 10 CPI print arrives seven days before the committee meets. If that number approaches 4%, the Fed won't have the luxury of waiting for more data — the June 17 meeting becomes a live hike decision."

The next major data event is Thursday, May 28, when the Bureau of Economic Analysis releases both the second estimate for Q1 2026 GDP and the April PCE price index. The advance GDP figure of +2.0% annualized is unlikely to be revised dramatically, but April PCE — the inflation gauge the Fed weights most heavily — carries outsized significance. March PCE ran at 3.5% year-over-year. Any acceleration in April toward 3.7% or beyond would narrow the already thin distance between the Fed's stated tolerance and the policy adjustment markets now view as probable. The PCE data will land twenty days before the June 17 decision — time enough to meaningfully shift the probability distribution of outcomes.

The June 5 Employment Situation report for May adds another variable to the calculus. April's 115,000 payroll print — below the prior year's trend but orderly — is consistent with the Fed's intended soft-landing trajectory. A second consecutive sub-150,000 reading would confirm the labor market is cooling, but it will not resolve the inflation question. The Fed cannot look past 3.8% CPI simply because payroll growth is decelerating; the dual mandate cuts both ways, and price stability currently commands the higher weight. With retail sales growing at 4.9% year-over-year and M2 at $22.7 trillion expanding at 4.6%, the monetary and consumer underpinnings of sustained inflation remain intact.

The June 10 CPI release — arriving just seven days before the committee meets — is the axis on which the Fed's entire second half playbook turns. If the Iran conflict's energy pass-through continues filtering into transportation, services, and shelter costs, a reading at or above 4% is a credible scenario. At that level, the conversation shifts decisively from whether the Fed can cut to whether it will hike. The monetary fuel for persistent price pressure is visible: M2 growth at 4.6% means aggregate purchasing power is expanding faster than the economy's productive capacity, a condition that historically sustains inflation even as underlying demand moderates.

What distinguishes this moment from the 2022–2023 tightening cycle is that the economy is not clearly overheating. Growth at 2.0%, unemployment at 4.3%, and consumer spending that, while robust, is not surging — these are not the conditions that typically demand emergency tightening. But neither is inflation at 3.8%, with an oil shock of uncertain duration keeping upside risks alive. The Federal Reserve engineered a soft landing once before, threading the needle between growth and price stability with remarkable precision. Whether it can do so again — with rates already at 3.63%, fiscal deficits widening, and a geopolitical shock outside its control — is the defining question of 2026. The answer begins arriving on May 28.
