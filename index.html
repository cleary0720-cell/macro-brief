Key Economic Indicators
JULY 2026
Monetary Policy
Federal Funds Rate
3.63%
ON HOLD →
The FOMC voted unanimously 12-0 on June 17 to hold the target range at 3.50–3.75% — the fifth consecutive hold and the first full decision cycle under new Chair Kevin Warsh. The June dot plot remains hawkish: nine of 18 members project at least one hike before year-end. The July 29 meeting is now 10 days away. Following a softer-than-expected June CPI report (3.5% vs. 3.9% expected), markets dramatically repriced: hike odds collapsed from 25% to 10%, and hold probability surged to 90%. The next rate decision will be shaped by whether Core PCE and labor market data corroborate the June CPI improvement.
In plain termsThe Fed held rates unchanged on June 17, and the July 29 decision is now 10 days away. After the June CPI report came in well below expectations (3.5% vs. 3.9% expected), markets repriced: hike odds fell from 25% to just 10%, and the base case is now a 90% probability the Fed holds again. Mortgage rates, car loans, and credit cards are unlikely to move before the July 29 meeting — and a hold decision changes nothing for borrowing costs.
Inflation ⚠ WATCH
Consumer Price Index (YoY)
3.5%
EASING ↓
June 2026 CPI rose 3.5% year-over-year — the first meaningful decline since the Iran-driven energy surge began, falling sharply from May's 4.2% and beating the 3.9% consensus forecast. Energy prices fell 5.7% month-over-month in June (the largest single-month decline since April 2020), pulling the annual energy reading from 23.5% to 15.7%. Core CPI, excluding food and energy, eased from 2.9% to 2.6% — the lowest core reading since early 2025, and below the 2.8% forecast. Shelter costs decelerated to 3.3% YoY. The report effectively ended market speculation about a July 29 rate hike: CME FedWatch moved from 25% hike probability to 10%, with hold at 90%. The June data is the first genuine disinflationary signal of 2026.
In plain termsInflation fell meaningfully in June — from 4.2% to 3.5% — largely because gasoline and energy prices dropped sharply from Iran-related highs. Strip out energy, and core prices are up just 2.6%, the best reading in over a year. Groceries, rent, and services are still more expensive than a year ago, but the pace of increase is slowing. This June CPI report almost certainly means the Fed will not hike rates on July 29. Mortgage rates and borrowing costs are unlikely to rise in the near term, though they remain historically elevated.
Labor Market
Unemployment Rate
4.2%
MISLEADING DECLINE ↓
June payrolls added just 57,000 jobs — the weakest monthly print in years, barely half the consensus estimate of 115,000, and a dramatic reversal from May's 172,000. The unemployment rate technically fell from 4.3% to 4.2%, but the mechanism was troubling: labor force participation dropped 0.3 percentage points to 61.5%, the lowest since March 2021. Workers are exiting the labor force, not finding new jobs. Leisure and hospitality shed 61,000 positions in June, while professional and business services and social assistance provided the only meaningful offsets. Average hourly earnings rose 0.3% month-over-month ($37.64), consistent with the prior trend. The headline rate improved for the wrong reason.
In plain termsThe unemployment rate dropped to 4.2%, but the underlying story is weaker than it looks. Only 57,000 new jobs were created in June — roughly half of what economists expected — and the rate fell mainly because 300,000 people stopped looking for work entirely (the lowest participation rate since March 2021). If you're job-hunting right now, the labor market is getting meaningfully harder. The Fed is watching carefully, but markets still price only a 24% chance of a July cut.
Economic Output
GDP Growth Rate (Q1 2026, Final Est.)
+2.1%
EXPANDING ▲
The BEA's third and final estimate for Q1 2026 GDP, released June 25, revised growth up to +2.1% annualized — a full 0.5 percentage point above the +1.6% second estimate and above the original +2.0% advance reading. The upward revision was driven primarily by a downward adjustment to imports (which subtract from GDP), partially offset by a modest downward revision to consumer spending. Q1 2026 now stands as the strongest quarterly growth reading since Q2 2025's +2.8%, confirming that the economy entered 2026 on solid footing despite tariff headwinds and geopolitical uncertainty.
In plain termsGDP measures the total output of the US economy. The final Q1 2026 reading of +2.1% — revised UP from the initial +1.6% estimate — shows the economy was growing faster than initially thought at the start of the year. No recession signal here. Paradoxically, strong GDP growth may help the Fed cut rates: a healthy economy doesn't need emergency-level monetary restraint, and with the labor market showing early softening signals, the Fed has room to ease without triggering overheating.
Fixed Income
10-Year Treasury Yield
4.55%
ELEVATED →
The 10-year Treasury yield held at 4.55% as of July 17 — essentially unchanged from last week despite a volatile week in bond markets. Yields spiked dramatically on July 13 as Iran-related oil price fears pushed hike odds to 46.5%, but the softer-than-expected June CPI report (3.5% vs. 3.9% expected) triggered an equally swift reversal. The 2-year yield eased slightly to 4.18%, narrowing the 2s10s spread marginally to +37 basis points. The 30-year rose to 5.09%. With hike odds collapsing from 25% to 10% following the CPI data, yields across the curve found their footing. The key question for the 10-year now: does the disinflation story hold through the September meeting?
In plain termsThe 10-year Treasury yield sets the floor for mortgage rates. At 4.55%, a 30-year fixed mortgage likely costs around 7.0–7.2% — meaning a $400,000 home loan runs approximately $2,660–$2,710 per month in principal and interest. The good news: after a wild spike on Iran fears earlier this week, yields settled back. With hike odds now at just 10% for July 29, mortgage rates are unlikely to climb further before the FOMC decision. Relief remains elusive, but the worst-case rate hike scenario has receded significantly.
Consumer Activity
Retail Sales Growth (YoY)
6.7%
EXPANDING ↑
June 2026 retail and food services sales reached $768.6 billion — up 6.7% year-over-year and 0.2% month-over-month, the fifth consecutive monthly gain. The softer monthly pace reflects lower gasoline station receipts (down 5.3% on cheaper pump prices), partially offset by solid gains in nonstore retailers and sporting goods. Total sales for April through June are up 6.4% from a year ago. The slight deceleration from May's 6.9% annual rate is not alarming — gasoline deflation mechanically pulls the number lower — and core retail sales (excluding autos and gas) remain robust. Released July 16, the data confirm that consumers continued spending through June's labor market softness.
In plain termsAmericans kept spending solidly in June — retail sales grew 6.7% year-over-year, the fifth straight monthly gain. The slight dip from May's 6.9% is largely explained by cheaper gas prices pulling down receipts at gas stations. Strip that out and consumer spending remains healthy. Even with a weak June jobs report (+57,000 payrolls) and elevated borrowing costs, consumers haven't pulled back — a good sign for economic growth, though it also gives the Fed less incentive to rush toward rate cuts.
Money Supply
M2 Money Supply Growth (YoY)
5.6%
ACCELERATING ↑
M2 expanded 5.6% year-over-year through May 2026, per the Federal Reserve's H.6 release published June 23 — up sharply from April's 4.7% and the fastest growth rate since the post-pandemic reflation era. Total money supply now stands at approximately $23.1 trillion. The near-one-percentage-point monthly acceleration is the week's most underappreciated development: historically, M2 expansion leads inflation by 12–18 months, meaning today's money supply surge could sustain elevated price levels well into 2027 even if energy-driven CPI moderates in the near term.
In plain termsM2 measures all the money circulating through the economy — cash, checking accounts, savings, and money market funds. The jump from 4.7% to 5.6% growth in a single month is significant: more money chasing the same goods is the textbook recipe for sustained inflation. Even if gas prices cool and headline CPI dips from 4.2%, this accelerating money supply is a structural force that could keep everyday prices elevated through 2027 — complicating any Fed pivot toward cuts.
Inflation
Core PCE Price Index (YoY)
3.4%
ELEVATED →
May 2026 Core PCE — the Fed's preferred inflation gauge — held at 3.4% year-over-year (BEA, released June 25), having re-accelerated from a 2.6% cycle low in early 2025. June data will be released in late July. The metric is 1.4 percentage points above the Fed's 2% target and has been climbing for 14 months — the primary reason the FOMC's June dot plot was hawkish. This week's June CPI report (core at 2.6%) is potentially encouraging news: CPI and PCE tend to trend together, and the CPI deceleration suggests June Core PCE may ease from the May reading. If confirmed, it would mark the first genuine turning point in the re-acceleration trend — and reshape the September FOMC calculus.
In plain termsCore PCE is the Fed's preferred inflation yardstick — it strips out food and energy to measure the underlying pace of price increases. At 3.4%, it's well above the 2% target. The Fed has said it needs to see sustained progress toward 2% before cutting rates. This week's June CPI (core at 2.6%) is an encouraging signal — if Core PCE follows the same trend in late July, it could suggest the disinflation that consumers need is finally beginning. For now, borrowing costs stay high until the Fed sees several months of improvement, not just one.
Economic Output
ISM Manufacturing PMI
53.3
EXPANDING ▲
June 2026 ISM Manufacturing PMI came in at 53.3 — down 0.7 percentage points from May's four-year high of 54.0, but the sixth consecutive month of expansion and broadly consistent with roughly 2% real GDP growth on an annualized basis. New orders remained strong at 56.0%, and the Prices Index posted the largest single-month decline since July 2022 (falling 9.1 points to 73%), a genuine disinflation signal from the industrial side of the economy. After bottoming at 47.9 in December 2025, manufacturing has now expanded for six straight months. The modest sequential deceleration reflects sustained momentum, not a reversal.
In plain termsA monthly survey of factory purchasing managers — any reading above 50 means manufacturing is growing, below 50 means it's shrinking. At 53.3, U.S. factories are still expanding at a healthy pace, even if slightly slower than May's four-year high. The good news: factory input prices fell sharply in June, which could eventually translate into lower prices at stores. Six consecutive months of expansion is a genuine recovery signal.
Labor Market
Initial Jobless Claims (4-wk avg)
214k
EASING ↓
The 4-week moving average of initial jobless claims fell to 214,250 for the week ending July 11, 2026 — reported July 17 — down 4,500 from the prior week's average of 218,750. This is the lowest 4-week average in the current cycle. The continued decline reinforces the "slow-hire, no-fire" characterization of the labor market: employers are retaining workers even as new hiring has stalled after June's +57,000 payroll miss. At 214k, the 4-week average sits well below the 260–280k range historically associated with recessionary conditions, providing a stabilizing signal ahead of the July 29 FOMC decision.
In plain termsEvery week, the government counts how many people filed for unemployment benefits for the first time. At 214,000 (4-week average) — the lowest reading in the current cycle — the pace of layoffs is improving. Companies are not firing workers even as new hiring has slowed dramatically. If you already have a job, you're in a relatively secure position. For those looking, the market is tight — fewer new openings — but stable. Lower jobless claims also reduce pressure on the Fed to cut rates as an emergency measure, supporting the "hold" consensus for July 29.

The CPI Break: Inflation Falls to 3.5%, Hike Odds Collapse, and Markets Begin Pricing Relief

CPI 3.5% (vs. 3.9% expected), core 2.6%, hike odds fall to 10%, jobless claims hit cycle low 214k — By Connor Leary, July 19, 2026

The week that began with the highest rate-hike probability of 2026 ended with its most significant disinflationary data point of the year. On Monday, July 13, Iranian port tensions sent oil prices surging more than 5% in a single session, pushing CME FedWatch hike odds to 46.5% for the July 29 FOMC meeting — the most hawkish pricing since the June dot plot. By Thursday, July 17, after June CPI printed at 3.5% year-over-year versus a 3.9% consensus — the largest favorable inflation surprise of 2026 — hike odds had collapsed to 10% and hold probability had surged to 90%. The week was, in miniature, a portrait of the entire 2026 macro narrative: geopolitical shock, inflation spike, abrupt reversal, and a Fed that has yet to move.

The mechanics of the June CPI decline are important to understand. Energy prices fell 5.7% on a monthly basis — the largest single-month decline since April 2020 — as the acute phase of the Iran supply disruption partially unwound. On a year-over-year basis, energy is still up 15.7%, a sharp improvement from May's 23.5% but still historically elevated. The more consequential number is core CPI, which excludes food and energy and fell from 2.9% to 2.6% — the lowest reading since early 2025, and 30 basis points below the 2.8% consensus. Shelter CPI eased to 3.3% from 3.4%, a modest but directionally welcome signal. The June report does not declare victory over inflation; it establishes that the re-acceleration trend that dominated the first half of 2026 may have peaked.

"From 46.5% hike odds on Monday to 10% by Friday — the June CPI reversed in one data release what took months of anxiety to build."

Retail sales for June came in at +6.7% year-over-year — slightly below May's 6.9% — released July 16. The monthly decline is largely mechanical: gasoline station receipts fell 5.3% as pump prices dropped, reducing the nominal spending total without reflecting a shift in consumer behavior. Nonstore retailers and sporting goods posted solid gains. The five-month streak of positive monthly retail readings confirms that consumers have absorbed the dual shock of 4.2% CPI and weak June payrolls (+57,000) without materially pulling back on spending. On the labor side, the 4-week moving average of initial jobless claims fell to 214,250 for the week ending July 11 — the lowest reading in the current cycle, down from 218,750 the prior week. The "slow-hire, no-fire" dynamic remains intact.

The July 29 FOMC meeting is now 10 days away, and the path is substantially clearer than it was seven days ago. With hike odds at 10% and hold at 90%, the base case is a unanimous hold at 3.50–3.75%. The more consequential signal will be in Chair Warsh's written statement (there is no press conference this meeting): will the FOMC soften its hawkish bias in light of the June CPI improvement, or will it point to still-elevated Core PCE (3.4% as of May, with June data due late July) as reason to keep all options open for September? Markets are beginning to price a different question — not whether September brings a hike, but whether it could bring the first rate cut of the cycle.

For households, the week's developments carry direct practical significance. Gasoline prices fell noticeably in June — the average American driver spent less at the pump. Core inflation at 2.6% means everyday goods and services are still rising above the Fed's target, but the pace has meaningfully improved. The 10-year Treasury held at 4.55%, keeping 30-year fixed mortgage rates in the 7.0–7.2% range — there is no near-term relief on home financing. The real consumer opportunity lies in what happens next: if June Core PCE (due late July) corroborates the CPI trend, the September meeting could become the Fed's first genuine turning point. The GDP Q2 advance estimate (July 30) will provide the other critical input. Two reports away, the macro picture could look dramatically different from where it stood just 10 days ago.
