Editor's Analysis

U.S. Economy Flashes Contraction as Trade Disruption Obscures the True Picture

A split-screen economy: resilient consumers and cooling inflation set against a contracting GDP print and persistent policy uncertainty — By Connor Leary, April 29, 2025

The first quarter of 2025 produced a headline number that will dominate financial conversations for months to come: a negative GDP print of −0.3%, the first contraction in three years. Yet beneath that alarming figure lies a more complicated story — one of genuine economic resilience colliding head-on with policy-induced statistical noise.

The contraction is not a simple demand collapse. Much of the Q1 drag originated from a massive surge in imports as businesses aggressively front-ran anticipated tariff escalations, a mechanical accounting effect that reduces GDP without reflecting any deterioration in underlying conditions. Domestic final sales — a far cleaner window into real economic momentum — remained modestly positive throughout the quarter.

"The consumer remains the last line of defense. Retail sales up 4.6% year-over-year signal that the spending engine has not yet stalled — and may be the most important data point of the quarter."

Inflation, for its part, continues its gradual convergence toward the Federal Reserve's 2% target. At 2.4%, CPI records its lowest print since early 2021. The disinflation trend is intact, and shelter inflation — long the most stubborn component of the index — is finally showing meaningful deceleration in major metros. The last mile to target remains treacherous, but the path is visible.

This backdrop gives Fed Chair Powell cover to maintain the current pause in the rate cycle. The FOMC faces a genuine bind: growth is wobbling, but inflation has not yet reached target. Cutting prematurely risks reigniting price pressures that proved remarkably difficult to extinguish; holding too long threatens to amplify any demand slowdown into something more durable.

The 10-Year Treasury yield at 4.17% reflects this tension precisely. Fixed income markets remain undecided — unable to commit to either a growth-fear rally or an inflation-persistence sell-off. The yield curve has steepened slightly from its historic inversion, a tentative signal of normalization, though hardly a confident one. Bond investors are watching the same data everyone else is, and reaching similarly inconclusive judgements.

M2 money supply growing at 3.9% is among the more quietly encouraging data points in this edition. After contracting through much of 2023 — an unprecedented event in postwar American economic history — the money supply is now expanding again at a pace that supports nominal growth without threatening to reflate the economy. It represents a narrow but real success for the central bank's long and painful tightening campaign.
