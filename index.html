Key Economic Indicators
JUNE 2026
Monetary Policy
Federal Funds Rate
3.63%
ON HOLD →
The FOMC held its target range at 3.50–3.75% for the fourth consecutive meeting — and markets price 99% probability of another hold at the June 17, 2026 decision. May CPI came in at 4.2% on June 10, driven by a 23.5% energy surge, but the energy-driven nature of the print has persuaded markets this is a supply shock rather than a demand signal requiring rate action. Core CPI at 2.9% and the dot plot's likely confirmation of zero 2026 cuts will be the real market-moving output on June 17.
In plain termsThe Fed sets the baseline cost of borrowing money. At 3.63%, mortgages, car loans, and credit cards remain expensive. The Fed almost certainly won't change rates on June 17 — but the dot plot (their rate outlook) could signal whether high rates are here through all of 2026 or just for a few more months.
Inflation ⚠ WATCH
Consumer Price Index (YoY)
4.2%
SURGING ↑
May 2026 CPI surged to 4.2% year-over-year — the highest reading since April 2023 — driven overwhelmingly by energy prices, which jumped 23.5% on an annual basis amid Iran-related geopolitical supply disruptions. Core CPI (excluding food and energy) came in at 2.9%, only modestly above the April figure of 2.8%, confirming that demand-driven price pressure remains relatively contained. Shelter costs rose 3.4% YoY, slightly above April's 3.3%. The 220-basis-point gap between headline CPI and the Fed's 2% target is the widest since early 2023 — but markets are pricing 99% probability of a hold on June 17, treating the energy spike as a supply shock rather than a demand signal requiring rate action.
In plain termsPrices are rising at 4.2% — more than twice the Fed's 2% goal — but most of that surge comes from a spike in gas and energy prices caused by the Iran-Israel conflict. Strip out energy and the picture is less alarming. Still, groceries, rent, and everyday expenses are all meaningfully more expensive than a year ago, and the Fed will need to see energy prices stabilize before inflation can trend back toward target.
Labor Market
Unemployment Rate
4.3%
STABLE →
May payrolls added 172,000 jobs — beating every economist estimate and the strongest monthly print since spring 2024 — with gains concentrated in leisure and hospitality, local government, and health care. The unemployment rate held at 4.3% for the third consecutive month, with 7.3 million unemployed and labor force participation steady at 61.8%. Upward revisions added 93,000 jobs to March and April combined. Average hourly earnings moderated to 3.4% year-over-year, a slight improvement that eases near-term wage-price spiral concerns — but the acceleration in hiring complicates the Fed's messaging ahead of the June 17 decision.
In plain termsThe job market is holding up better than most economists expected. With 172,000 jobs added in May and the unemployment rate steady at 4.3%, layoffs are not accelerating and most employed workers are keeping their jobs. The strong hiring pace actually makes it harder for the Fed to cut rates — a hot labor market means less economic slack and more inflationary pressure.
Economic Output
GDP Growth Rate (Q1 2026, 2nd Est.)
+1.6%
EXPANDING ▲
The BEA's second estimate for Q1 2026 GDP, released May 28, revised growth down to +1.6% annualized from the advance estimate of +2.0% — a 0.4 percentage point reduction driven primarily by weaker investment and consumer spending than initially measured. Exports and government spending provided partial offsets. The economy is still expanding and avoided contraction, but the revision signals that first-quarter momentum was softer than first reported.
In plain termsGDP measures the total output of the US economy. At +1.6% annualized growth in Q1 2026 — revised down from +2.0% — the economy is still expanding, but more modestly than first estimated. Businesses are still growing and hiring; there is no recession signal here, but the pace is decelerating.
Fixed Income
10-Year Treasury Yield
4.49%
ELEVATED →
The 10-year Treasury yield closed at 4.49% on June 12, easing from the June 9 peak of 4.56% that followed the strong May jobs report. The post-CPI relief trade drove yields lower after the June 10 print confirmed that the May inflation surge was energy-driven — with core CPI at only 2.9% — reducing the perceived urgency of further Fed tightening. The 2s10s spread widened to +40 basis points and the 3m10y spread sits at +78 basis points, indicating a normal, slightly steepening curve consistent with a higher-for-longer rate environment rather than imminent hikes.
In plain termsThe 10-year Treasury yield heavily influences mortgage rates. At 4.49%, a 30-year fixed mortgage likely costs around 7.0% — meaning a $400,000 home loan runs approximately $2,661 per month in principal and interest. Rates remain significantly higher than the sub-4% levels that prevailed before the Fed's tightening cycle, keeping home affordability stretched for most buyers.
Consumer Activity
Retail Sales Growth (YoY)
4.9%
ACCELERATING ↑
April 2026 advance retail and food services sales rose 4.9% year-over-year — accelerating from March's 4.0% — totaling $757.1 billion. Nonstore retailers surged 11.1% as e-commerce continued to capture share. Month-over-month, sales rose 0.5%. Total sales for February through April are up 4.4% from the same period a year ago.
In plain termsAmericans stepped up their spending in April, growing faster than inflation. This is the economy's strongest positive signal: consumers are not pulling back despite high prices, rising yields, and global uncertainty.
Money Supply
M2 Money Supply Growth (YoY)
4.5%
MODERATING ↓
M2 expanded 4.5% year-over-year through April 2026, per the Fed's May 26 H.6 release — revised from an earlier estimate of 4.7% and up from March's 3.9% annualized growth rate. Total money supply stands at approximately $21.9 trillion, reflecting the Fed's continued quantitative tightening alongside residual QE. The deceleration in the pace of M2 growth from recent peaks is a modest positive for the inflation outlook, though 4.5% annual growth still sits comfortably above the 2–3% range historically associated with price stability and provides structural support for elevated prices.
In plain termsM2 measures all the money circulating through the economy — cash, checking accounts, savings, and money market funds. At 4.5% annual growth, there is still more money chasing goods than the economy's productive capacity can absorb — a key reason inflation hasn't returned to the Fed's 2% target despite high borrowing costs.
Inflation
Core PCE Price Index (YoY)
3.3%
RE-ACCELERATING ↑
April 2026 Core PCE — the Fed's preferred inflation gauge — rose 3.3% year-over-year, up from 3.2% in March and well above the 2% target. After bottoming near 2.6% in early 2025, core prices have climbed steadily for more than a year as service-sector and shelter costs prove persistent.
In plain termsThe Fed's favorite inflation yardstick — it strips out food and energy to measure the "core" of price pressure. At 3.3%, it's well above their 2% goal and has been creeping higher for over a year. This is the number that most directly influences whether the Fed raises or cuts interest rates.
Economic Output
ISM Manufacturing PMI
54.0
EXPANDING ▲
May 2026 ISM Manufacturing PMI surged to 54.0 — the strongest reading since May 2022 — capping a dramatic reversal from 10 consecutive months of contraction through 2025. After bottoming at 47.9 in December 2025, manufacturing has expanded for five straight months as new orders and production accelerate.
In plain termsA monthly survey of factory purchasing managers — any reading above 50 means manufacturing is growing, below 50 means it's shrinking. At 54.0, U.S. factories are expanding at their fastest pace in four years. Strong manufacturing usually signals more jobs and higher wages ahead.
Labor Market
Initial Jobless Claims (4-wk avg)
219k
EDGING HIGHER ↑
The 4-week moving average of initial jobless claims rose to 219,000 for the week ending June 6, 2026 — up from 214,750 the prior week and above the cycle low of approximately 200,000 reached in early May. The uptick mirrors the normal seasonal variation following Memorial Day weekend and does not yet signal a deterioration in labor market conditions. At 219,000, claims remain well within the healthy historical range and consistent with a labor market still generating 172,000 new jobs per month.
In plain termsEvery week, the government counts how many people filed for unemployment benefits for the first time. At 219,000, layoffs are rising slightly off recent lows but remain low by historical standards. The uptick is too small to signal a labor market weakening — it simply reflects normal seasonal patterns after a holiday. For the Fed, still-low claims remove pressure to cut rates.

Editor's Analysis

May CPI Hits 4.2% on Iran Energy Shock — a Strong Jobs Report Complicates the June 17 Hold

Markets price 99% for a hold, but the dot plot — and Powell's tone — may deliver the real shock on June 17 — By Connor Leary, June 14, 2026

The Bureau of Labor Statistics delivered back-to-back market-moving numbers within five trading days: May payrolls of +172,000 on June 5 — beating every economist estimate, with upward revisions adding 93,000 to March and April — followed by May CPI of 4.2% year-over-year on June 10, the highest headline reading since April 2023. The juxtaposition is almost cartoonishly difficult for the Federal Reserve: a labor market re-accelerating rather than softening, paired with inflation 220 basis points above the 2% target. Yet the market has reached near-consensus on a hold at June 17, with CME FedWatch showing 99% probability of no change — because the decomposition of the CPI data tells a more nuanced story than the headline suggests.

Energy prices are the dominant driver. The energy component rose 23.5% year-over-year — a dramatic acceleration from April's 17.9% — as geopolitical supply disruptions from the Iran-Israel conflict continued feeding through to US gasoline and natural gas prices. Strip energy out, and core CPI was 2.9% in May, only modestly above April's 2.8% and decelerating from the 3.4–3.6% readings seen in early 2026. The Fed's institutional reluctance to respond to energy-driven inflation — always treated as a supply shock outside the scope of monetary policy — has evidently persuaded markets that June 17 is a clean hold. As one sell-side strategist noted after the CPI release: "The 4.2% headline is alarming, but the Fed reads the same decomposition as the market: energy is the driver, and energy price shocks are not what rate hikes fix."

"The 4.2% headline is alarming, but the Fed reads the same decomposition as the market: energy is the driver, and energy price shocks are not what rate hikes fix."

Yet the labor market complicates the complacency narrative. May's 172,000 payroll print represented a re-acceleration from April's revised 179,000 — and when combined with the upward revisions, the three-month average sits well above the break-even rate needed to absorb new labor force entrants. Average hourly earnings moderated to 3.4% year-over-year from April's 3.6%, which is the most encouraging data point for the wage-price spiral thesis — but still above the 2.5–3% range the Fed associates with durable 2% inflation. A labor market that is tightening rather than cooling removes one of the cleaner arguments for maintaining any residual easing bias.

The 10-year Treasury yield has been the most sensitive real-time barometer of this data collision. After touching 4.56% on June 9 — reflecting hot payroll-driven inflation expectations — yields fell to 4.49% on June 12 as the energy-driven CPI interpretation gained traction. The 2s10s spread sits at +40 basis points and the 3m10y at +78 basis points — a curve that is normal and modestly steepening, consistent with higher-for-longer rather than rate hikes. That distinction — higher-for-longer versus more hikes — is precisely what the committee will need to communicate on June 17. The former is already priced; the latter is not, and even a subtle shift in language toward openness to rate increases could reprice the entire front end of the curve.

The more consequential June 17 output may not be the rate decision itself but the Summary of Economic Projections — the dot plot. If a majority of FOMC members now project zero rate cuts for 2026, or if a meaningful minority has shifted toward projecting a rate increase, the dot plot will have done more work than the decision. The market currently prices a 76.8% probability of zero cuts in all of 2026. A dot plot confirming that median view removes the ambiguity and shifts the debate to 2027. A dot plot that surprises with hike projections — even one or two members — would be the equivalent of a hawkish shock in a market that has definitively moved to pricing higher-for-longer rather than higher-still.

Against this backdrop, the consumer side of the economy remains the clearest structural argument against pessimism. April retail sales of $757.1 billion — up 4.9% year-over-year — confirm that elevated prices have not yet broken consumer spending. May ISM Manufacturing at 54.0 signals the strongest factory activity in four years. And 172,000 May jobs confirm that the labor market, whatever its nuances, is still absorbing workers at an above-trend pace. The data challenge facing the Fed is not an economy in distress but an economy that continues to run hot enough — in labor, in consumption, and now in headline inflation — to make patience at 3.50–3.75% the only defensible policy choice. The June 17 press conference will define whether the Fed frames that patience as temporary prudence or durable commitment.
